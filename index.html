The Tree of Life

Three Domains

  • Bacteria: prokaryotic, peptidoglycan cell walls, most diverse metabolically
  • Archaea: prokaryotic, no peptidoglycan, many extremophiles, more closely related to Eukarya
  • Eukarya: eukaryotic (membrane-bound nucleus and organelles). Includes protists, fungi, plants, animals.

Phylogenetics Basics

  • A phylogeny is a hypothesis about evolutionary relationships
  • Clade: a group consisting of an ancestor and ALL its descendants (monophyletic)
  • Sister taxa: two groups that share the most recent common ancestor
  • Outgroup: a taxon outside the group being studied, used to root the tree
  • Derived trait (synapomorphy): a shared trait that evolved in the common ancestor of a clade

How to Read a Phylogenetic Tree

  • Branch tips = current taxa (living or extinct)
  • Nodes (branch points) = common ancestors
  • The root = the most ancient common ancestor on the tree
  • Closeness of relationship is determined by how recently two taxa share a common ancestor, NOT by how close they appear on the page
  • Branches can be rotated at any node without changing the meaning

Classification Reminders

Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

"Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Spaghetti"

Binomial nomenclature: Genus species (italicized, genus capitalized). Example: Homo sapiens.

Key Evolutionary Transitions

  • Prokaryote → Eukaryote (endosymbiosis)
  • Unicellular → Multicellular
  • Water → Land (plants, then animals)
  • Invertebrate → Vertebrate
  • Ectotherm → Endotherm
  • Egg-laying → Live birth

Bacteria & Archaea

Bacterial Cell Structure

  • Prokaryotic: no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles
  • Circular DNA in nucleoid region, plus plasmids (small extra DNA rings)
  • 70S ribosomes (smaller than eukaryotic 80S)
  • Cell wall contains peptidoglycan
  • Many have flagella (motility), pili (attachment/conjugation), capsule (protection)

Gram Stain

  • Gram-positive: thick peptidoglycan wall, stains purple/violet
  • Gram-negative: thin peptidoglycan wall + outer membrane, stains pink/red
  • Gram-negative bacteria are generally harder to treat with antibiotics because of the outer membrane

Bacterial Shapes

  • Coccus (cocci): spherical
  • Bacillus (bacilli): rod-shaped
  • Spirillum (spirilla): spiral/corkscrew

Bacterial Reproduction & Genetics

  • Reproduce by binary fission (asexual, fast)
  • Genetic variation through: mutation, conjugation (plasmid transfer via pili), transformation (uptake of free DNA), transduction (virus-mediated transfer)

Metabolic Diversity

  • Photoautotrophs: use light energy, fix CO₂ (cyanobacteria)
  • Chemoautotrophs: use chemical energy from inorganic molecules
  • Photoheterotrophs: use light, consume organic compounds
  • Chemoheterotrophs: use chemical energy from organic compounds (most bacteria, all animals)
  • Obligate aerobes: need O₂. Obligate anaerobes: O₂ is toxic. Facultative anaerobes: can live with or without O₂.

Archaea vs. Bacteria

  • Both prokaryotic, but Archaea lack peptidoglycan
  • Archaea have unique membrane lipids (ether-linked, branched)
  • Archaea are more closely related to Eukarya than to Bacteria
  • Many Archaea are extremophiles: thermophiles (heat), halophiles (salt), methanogens (produce methane)
  • No known archaeal pathogens

Protists

What Are Protists?

Protists are eukaryotes that are NOT plants, animals, or fungi. They are an informal, paraphyletic group (not a true clade). They include unicellular, colonial, and simple multicellular organisms. They are incredibly diverse in nutrition, reproduction, and habitat.

Animal-like Protists (Protozoans)

  • Heterotrophic, mostly motile
  • Amoebozoans: move by pseudopods (e.g., Amoeba)
  • Ciliates: move by cilia (e.g., Paramecium)
  • Flagellates: move by flagella (e.g., Trypanosoma, causes sleeping sickness)
  • Apicomplexans: parasitic, no motility in adult form (e.g., Plasmodium, causes malaria)

Plant-like Protists (Algae)

  • Photosynthetic, contain chloroplasts
  • Green algae: closest relatives of land plants, chlorophyll a and b
  • Red algae: phycoerythrin pigment, mostly marine
  • Brown algae: includes kelp, largest protists, fucoxanthin pigment
  • Diatoms: glass-like silica cell walls, major producers in oceans
  • Dinoflagellates: two flagella, some cause red tides, some are bioluminescent

Fungus-like Protists

  • Slime molds: heterotrophic, decomposers
  • Plasmodial slime molds: one giant multinucleate cell
  • Cellular slime molds: individual cells that aggregate when food is scarce
  • Water molds (Oomycetes): filamentous, cellulose cell walls (not chitin). Phytophthora caused the Irish potato famine.

Fungi

Defining Traits of Fungi

  • Eukaryotic, mostly multicellular (yeasts are unicellular)
  • Heterotrophic by absorption (secrete enzymes externally, absorb nutrients)
  • Cell walls made of chitin (not cellulose)
  • Body is made of hyphae (filaments); a mass of hyphae = mycelium
  • Store energy as glycogen (like animals, not starch like plants)
  • More closely related to animals than to plants

Fungal Reproduction

  • Reproduce by spores (sexual and asexual)
  • Asexual: budding (yeasts), fragmentation, or asexual spore production
  • Sexual: involves fusion of hyphae from two mating types, formation of a dikaryon (two nuclei per cell), then fusion and meiosis to produce spores

Ecological Roles of Fungi

  • Decomposers: break down dead organic matter, recycle nutrients (especially important for carbon and nitrogen cycling)
  • Mycorrhizae: mutualistic association between fungi and plant roots. Fungi get sugars; plants get increased water and mineral absorption. Found in ~90% of plant species.
  • Lichens: mutualistic partnership between a fungus and a photosynthetic organism (alga or cyanobacterium). Pioneer species on bare rock.
  • Pathogens: athlete's foot, ringworm, Dutch elm disease, chytrid fungus in amphibians

Plants

Defining Traits of Plants

  • Eukaryotic, multicellular, photoautotrophic
  • Cell walls made of cellulose
  • Chloroplasts with chlorophyll a and b
  • Store energy as starch
  • Evolved from green algae (charophytes are closest living relatives)
  • Alternation of generations: life cycle alternates between haploid gametophyte and diploid sporophyte

Challenges of Living on Land

  • Desiccation: solved by waxy cuticle, stomata for gas exchange
  • Support against gravity: solved by lignin, vascular tissue
  • Transport of water/nutrients: solved by xylem and phloem
  • Reproduction without water: solved by pollen and seeds
  • UV radiation: solved by protective compounds

Evolutionary trend: gametophyte generation becomes smaller and more dependent on the sporophyte as you move from bryophytes to angiosperms.

Alternation of Generations

  • Plants alternate between two multicellular stages:
  • Sporophyte (2n): diploid, produces spores by meiosis
  • Gametophyte (n): haploid, produces gametes (egg, sperm) by mitosis
  • In bryophytes, the gametophyte is dominant (the green part you see)
  • In ferns, gymnosperms, and angiosperms, the sporophyte is dominant

Animal Diversity Overview

Defining Traits of Animals

  • Eukaryotic, multicellular, heterotrophic (ingest food)
  • No cell walls
  • Most have nervous tissue and muscle tissue
  • Most reproduce sexually with a diploid-dominant life cycle
  • Most are motile at some stage of life
  • Develop from a blastula (hollow ball of cells) during embryogenesis

Key Features for Classifying Animals

  • Symmetry: asymmetric (sponges), radial (cnidarians), bilateral (most animals)
  • Body cavity: acoelomate (no cavity), pseudocoelomate, coelomate (true coelom)
  • Germ layers: diploblastic (2 layers: ecto/endoderm) vs. triploblastic (3 layers: + mesoderm)
  • Development: protostome (mouth first) vs. deuterostome (anus first)
  • Segmentation: repeated body units (annelids, arthropods, chordates)
  • Cephalization: concentration of sensory organs at the head end

Arthropod Subgroups

  • Insects: 3 body segments (head, thorax, abdomen), 6 legs, most have wings. Largest group of animals on Earth.
  • Arachnids: 2 body segments (cephalothorax, abdomen), 8 legs. Spiders, scorpions, ticks.
  • Crustaceans: 2 body segments, variable leg number, mostly aquatic. Crabs, lobsters, shrimp, barnacles.
  • Myriapods: many segments, many legs. Centipedes, millipedes.

Mollusk Body Plan

  • Foot: muscular, used for movement
  • Visceral mass: contains internal organs
  • Mantle: covers visceral mass, secretes shell (if present)
  • Three main classes: Gastropods (snails, slugs), Bivalves (clams, mussels), Cephalopods (octopus, squid: closed circulatory system, most intelligent invertebrates)

Vertebrates

Phylum Chordata: 4 Defining Traits

All chordates have these four features at some point in development:

  • Notochord: flexible rod for support (replaced by vertebral column in vertebrates)
  • Dorsal hollow nerve cord (becomes brain and spinal cord)
  • Pharyngeal slits (become gills in fish, modified in other vertebrates)
  • Post-anal tail

The Amniotic Egg

The amniotic egg was a key adaptation for life on land, freeing reptiles, birds, and mammals from needing water to reproduce.

  • Amnion: fluid-filled sac protecting the embryo
  • Chorion: gas exchange
  • Allantois: waste storage
  • Yolk sac: nutrition
  • Shell (in reptiles and birds): prevents desiccation

Mammal Subgroups

  • Monotremes: egg-laying mammals (platypus, echidna)
  • Marsupials: pouch for developing young (kangaroo, opossum)
  • Eutherians (placentals): long internal development via placenta (humans, dogs, whales). Largest group of mammals.

Ectotherm vs. Endotherm

  • Ectotherm: body temperature depends on environment ("cold-blooded"). Fish, amphibians, reptiles. Lower metabolic rate, need less food.
  • Endotherm: generate own body heat internally ("warm-blooded"). Birds, mammals. Higher metabolic rate, need more food, active in cold environments.

Plant Form & Function

Vascular Tissue

  • Xylem: transports water and minerals up from roots. Dead cells at maturity. One-way flow driven by transpiration pull.
  • Phloem: transports sugars (from photosynthesis) throughout the plant. Living cells at maturity. Bidirectional (source to sink).
"Xylem up, Phloem food"

Roots, Stems, Leaves

  • Roots: anchor plant, absorb water/minerals, store food. Root hairs increase surface area.
  • Stems: support, transport between roots and leaves. Contain vascular bundles.
  • Leaves: primary site of photosynthesis. Stomata (pores on underside) allow gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ out). Guard cells open/close stomata.

Transpiration & Water Movement

  • Transpiration: evaporation of water from leaves through stomata
  • Creates a pulling force (negative pressure) that draws water up through xylem
  • Transpiration-cohesion-tension mechanism: water molecules stick together (cohesion via H-bonds) and are pulled upward in a continuous column
  • Adhesion of water to xylem walls also helps

Plant Reproduction (Angiosperms)

  • Flower parts: sepals, petals, stamens (male: anther + filament), carpel/pistil (female: stigma, style, ovary)
  • Pollination: transfer of pollen from anther to stigma (by wind, insects, birds, etc.)
  • Double fertilization (unique to angiosperms): one sperm fertilizes the egg (zygote, 2n), the other fuses with two polar nuclei to form endosperm (3n, nutritive tissue)
  • Fruit: mature ovary that protects and disperses seeds

Animal Form & Function

Gas Exchange Across Animals

  • All gas exchange requires a moist surface and works by diffusion
  • Skin: earthworms, amphibians
  • Gills: fish, aquatic invertebrates. High surface area, countercurrent exchange maximizes O₂ uptake.
  • Tracheae: insects. Network of tubes delivering air directly to cells.
  • Lungs: terrestrial vertebrates. Alveoli in mammals provide huge surface area.

Circulatory Systems

  • Open: blood (hemolymph) bathes organs directly, no distinction between blood and interstitial fluid. Arthropods, most mollusks.
  • Closed: blood stays in vessels. Annelids, cephalopods, all vertebrates. More efficient for large, active animals.
  • Fish: 2-chambered heart (1 atrium, 1 ventricle), single circulation
  • Amphibians: 3-chambered heart (2 atria, 1 ventricle), some mixing
  • Birds/Mammals: 4-chambered heart (2 atria, 2 ventricles), complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood

Digestion Across Animals

  • Incomplete digestive tract: one opening (mouth = anus). Cnidarians, flatworms.
  • Complete digestive tract: two openings (mouth and anus), allows specialization. Roundworms onward.
  • Trend: increasing specialization of gut regions as animals become more complex

Reproduction: Key Terms

  • Asexual: budding (hydra), fragmentation (sea stars), parthenogenesis (some lizards, insects)
  • Sexual: requires gametes (sperm + egg), produces genetic diversity
  • External fertilization: eggs fertilized outside the body (most fish, amphibians). Requires water.
  • Internal fertilization: sperm deposited inside female (reptiles, birds, mammals). Allows reproduction on land.
  • Metamorphosis: dramatic change in body form during development (insects: complete vs. incomplete; amphibians: tadpole to adult)

Ecology

Population Ecology Essentials

  • Population: all individuals of one species in an area
  • Exponential growth (J-curve): unlimited resources, growth accelerates
  • Logistic growth (S-curve): growth slows as population approaches carrying capacity (K)
  • r-selected: many offspring, little care (insects, bacteria)
  • K-selected: few offspring, lots of care (elephants, humans)

Community Ecology

  • Community: all species in an area interacting together
  • Competition (−/−): both species harmed. Competitive exclusion principle: two species cannot occupy the same niche indefinitely.
  • Predation (+/−): one eats the other
  • Mutualism (+/+): both benefit (mycorrhizae, pollination)
  • Parasitism (+/−): parasite benefits, host harmed
  • Commensalism (+/0): one benefits, other unaffected
  • Keystone species: a species whose impact on the community is disproportionately large relative to its abundance

Energy Flow & Trophic Levels

  • Energy flows one way through ecosystems (not recycled)
  • 10% rule: only ~10% of energy transfers to the next trophic level
  • Producers (autotrophs) → Primary consumers → Secondary consumers → Tertiary consumers
  • Decomposers break down dead matter at all levels
  • This is why there are far fewer top predators than producers

Biodiversity & Conservation

  • Biodiversity: variety of life at all levels (genetic, species, ecosystem)
  • Major threats: habitat destruction (the biggest), overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, climate change
  • Invasive species: non-native species that outcompete natives (no natural predators)
  • Extinction is permanent. Current rates far exceed background extinction rates.
