01 — Tree of Life & Phylogenetics

All life is classified into three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. Bacteria and Archaea are prokaryotic (no membrane-bound nucleus), while Eukarya includes all organisms with membrane-bound nuclei and organelles. The three-domain system was proposed by Carl Woese based on ribosomal RNA sequence comparisons.

Reading a Phylogenetic Tree

A phylogenetic tree shows evolutionary relationships. The branch points (nodes) represent common ancestors. Sister taxa share the most recent common ancestor. An outgroup is the most distantly related taxon used to root the tree. Monophyletic groups (clades) include an ancestor and all its descendants. Paraphyletic groups include an ancestor but not all descendants. Polyphyletic groups do not include the common ancestor of all members.

Remember: "Mono = one ancestor, all kids. Para = one ancestor, missing kids. Poly = no shared ancestor at all."

Key Concepts

Homologous structures share a common evolutionary origin (bat wing vs. human arm). Analogous structures have similar function but different origins (bird wing vs. insect wing) and result from convergent evolution. Molecular phylogenetics uses DNA, RNA, or protein sequences to determine relationships. The more similar the sequences, the more closely related the organisms.

02 — Bacteria & Archaea

Prokaryotes are the most abundant organisms on Earth. They lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Their DNA is a single circular chromosome in the nucleoid region, and many carry small circular DNA molecules called plasmids. Ribosomes are 70S (compared to 80S in eukaryotes).

Bacterial Cell Structure

All bacteria have a plasma membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, and DNA. Most have a cell wall made of peptidoglycan. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan wall that retains the crystal violet stain (appears purple). Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer surrounded by an outer membrane with lipopolysaccharides (LPS), stain pink with safranin counterstain.

Gram stain procedure: Crystal violet → Iodine (mordant) → Alcohol decolorizer → Safranin (counterstain)

Bacterial Reproduction & Genetic Diversity

Bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission (one cell splits into two identical daughter cells). Genetic diversity comes from three mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer: transformation (uptake of free DNA from environment), transduction (transfer via bacteriophage), and conjugation (direct transfer through a pilus, requires F plasmid).

Remember horizontal gene transfer: "TransForMation = Free DNA. TransDuction = phage Does it. Conjugation = Contact (pilus)."

Archaea vs. Bacteria

Archaea are often found in extreme environments (extremophiles), though many live in moderate conditions. Key differences from bacteria: archaea lack peptidoglycan in their cell walls, have ether-linked membrane lipids (branched isoprene chains), and their RNA polymerase and ribosomal proteins are more similar to eukaryotes. Archaea are not known to cause human disease.

03 — Protists

Protists are eukaryotes that are not plants, animals, or fungi. They are an informal, polyphyletic group (meaning they do not share a single common ancestor exclusive to the group). Protists can be unicellular, colonial, or multicellular, and include autotrophs, heterotrophs, and mixotrophs.

Animal-like Protists (Protozoa)

Protozoa are heterotrophic and motile. Amoebas move by pseudopodia (cytoplasmic extensions). Ciliates like Paramecium use cilia for movement and feeding. Flagellates use one or more flagella. Apicomplexans are parasitic and have an apical complex for invading host cells. Plasmodium (causes malaria) is the most important apicomplexan, transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes.

Plant-like Protists (Algae)

Algae are photosynthetic protists. Green algae (Chlorophyta) are the closest relatives of land plants, sharing chlorophylls a and b plus starch storage. Red algae (Rhodophyta) have phycoerythrin pigment and can live at greater depths. Brown algae (Phaeophyta) include kelp and are the largest protists. Diatoms have glass-like silica cell walls. Dinoflagellates have two flagella and cellulose plates; some cause red tides and some are bioluminescent.

Fungus-like Protists

Slime molds and water molds superficially resemble fungi but are not closely related. Plasmodial slime molds form a large multinucleate mass called a plasmodium. Cellular slime molds live as individual amoeboid cells that aggregate when food is scarce. Oomycetes (water molds) have cellulose cell walls (not chitin like true fungi) and include Phytophthora infestans, which caused the Irish potato famine.

04 — Fungi

Fungi are eukaryotic heterotrophs that digest food externally by secreting enzymes and absorbing nutrients (absorptive heterotrophy). Cell walls are made of chitin. Most are multicellular except yeasts. The body of a multicellular fungus is called a mycelium, composed of thread-like hyphae. Hyphae can be septate (divided by cross-walls) or coenocytic (no septa, multinucleate).

Ecological Roles

Fungi are critical decomposers, breaking down dead organic matter and recycling nutrients. Mycorrhizae are mutualistic associations between fungi and plant roots. Ectomycorrhizae form a sheath around roots; arbuscular mycorrhizae penetrate root cell walls. About 90% of plant species associate with mycorrhizal fungi. Lichens are a mutualism between a fungus (usually ascomycete) and a photosynthetic partner (green alga or cyanobacterium). The fungus provides structure and moisture; the photosynthetic partner provides food.

Remember: "Fungi are not plants. They don't photosynthesize. Chitin walls, external digestion, absorptive nutrition."

05 — Plant Diversity

Plants are multicellular, photosynthetic eukaryotes with cell walls made of cellulose. They evolved from green algae (Charophytes are the closest living relatives). All plants exhibit alternation of generations, cycling between a diploid sporophyte and a haploid gametophyte.

Alternation of generations: Sporophyte (2n) → meiosis → spores → Gametophyte (n) → mitosis → gametes → fertilization → Sporophyte (2n)

Key Adaptations to Land

The move from water to land required several adaptations: a waxy cuticle to prevent desiccation, stomata for gas exchange, vascular tissue (xylem and phloem) for transport in taller plants, seeds for protection and dispersal in dry conditions, and pollen for fertilization without water. Bryophytes still require water for fertilization (swimming sperm) and have a dominant gametophyte generation. In vascular plants, the sporophyte is dominant.

06 — Animal Diversity & Body Plans

Animals are multicellular, heterotrophic eukaryotes that lack cell walls. Most animals reproduce sexually, and most are motile at some stage of life. Animals evolved from a colonial, flagellated protist ancestor.

Body Plan Features

Symmetry: animals are either asymmetrical (sponges), radially symmetrical (cnidarians, echinoderms), or bilaterally symmetrical (most animals). Bilateral symmetry is associated with cephalization (concentration of sensory organs at the head end).

Tissue layers: diploblastic animals have two germ layers (ectoderm and endoderm), like cnidarians. Triploblastic animals have three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm), which is the case for all bilaterians.

Body cavities: acoelomates have no body cavity (flatworms). Pseudocoelomates have a body cavity not fully lined by mesoderm (roundworms). Coelomates have a true coelom fully lined by mesoderm (annelids, chordates).

Ectoderm → skin, nervous system | Mesoderm → muscle, bone, circulatory | Endoderm → gut lining, lungs, liver

Mollusk Body Plan

All mollusks share three basic features: a muscular foot (for movement), a visceral mass (containing internal organs), and a mantle (tissue that may secrete a shell). Gastropods (snails, slugs) have a single shell or none. Bivalves (clams, mussels) have two hinged shells. Cephalopods (octopus, squid) have a reduced or absent shell, a closed circulatory system, and are the most intelligent invertebrates.

08 — Vertebrates

All vertebrates are chordates (Phylum Chordata). The four defining chordate features, present at some stage of life, are: a notochord, a dorsal hollow nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, and a post-anal tail. In vertebrates, the notochord is largely replaced by a vertebral column.

Amniotic Egg

The amniotic egg was a major adaptation allowing reproduction on land without water. It has four membranes: the amnion (fluid-filled sac cushioning the embryo), the chorion (gas exchange), the allantois (waste storage), and the yolk sac (nutrition). Reptiles, birds, and egg-laying mammals have shelled amniotic eggs. In placental mammals, these membranes are modified to form the placenta.

Mammal Subgroups

Monotremes lay eggs (platypus, echidna). Marsupials give birth to very undeveloped young that complete development in a pouch (kangaroo, opossum, koala). Placental mammals (eutherians) have long gestation with nourishment via a placenta and represent the vast majority of living mammal species.

Ectotherms vs. Endotherms

Ectotherms rely on external sources to regulate body temperature (fish, amphibians, reptiles). Endotherms generate their own body heat through metabolism (birds, mammals). Endothermy allows activity in cold environments but requires significantly more food/energy.

09 — Plant Form & Function

Vascular plants have two transport tissues: xylem carries water and dissolved minerals upward from roots (dead cells at maturity, tracheids and vessel elements), and phloem transports sugars (sucrose) from sources to sinks (living cells, sieve-tube elements with companion cells).

Transpiration-Cohesion-Tension: Water evaporates from stomata (transpiration) → creates tension → pulls water up xylem via cohesion (H-bonds between water molecules) and adhesion (water sticking to xylem walls)

Roots, Stems, Leaves

Roots absorb water and minerals via root hairs (extensions of epidermal cells). The Casparian strip in the endodermis forces water through cell membranes, providing selective control over what enters the vascular tissue. Stems provide support and contain vascular bundles. Leaves are the primary site of photosynthesis. Stomata are pores on leaves regulated by guard cells that open and close to balance gas exchange with water loss.

Flower Structure

A complete flower has four whorls: sepals (protect bud), petals (attract pollinators), stamens (male: anther produces pollen, filament supports anther), and carpels/pistils (female: stigma receives pollen, style connects to ovary, ovary contains ovules).

Double Fertilization (Angiosperms only)

One sperm fuses with the egg to form the zygote (2n). A second sperm fuses with two polar nuclei to form the endosperm (3n), which nourishes the developing embryo. This is unique to angiosperms. After fertilization, the ovule becomes the seed and the ovary becomes the fruit.

Ovule → Seed | Ovary → Fruit | Zygote (2n) + Endosperm (3n) = Double fertilization

10 — Animal Form & Function

Gas Exchange

All gas exchange requires a moist surface. Aquatic organisms use gills (large surface area, countercurrent exchange in fish maximizes O₂ extraction). Terrestrial insects use tracheae (branching tubes delivering air directly to cells). Terrestrial vertebrates use lungs. Amphibians supplement lung breathing with cutaneous (skin) respiration.

Circulatory Systems

Open circulatory systems (arthropods, most mollusks): hemolymph bathes organs directly in a hemocoel; no distinction between blood and interstitial fluid. Closed circulatory systems (annelids, cephalopods, vertebrates): blood confined to vessels, allowing higher pressure and more efficient delivery. Vertebrate heart evolution: 2-chambered (fish) → 3-chambered (amphibians, most reptiles) → 4-chambered (crocodilians, birds, mammals) with complete separation of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

Digestion

Incomplete digestive systems have one opening (cnidarians, flatworms). Complete digestive systems have a mouth and anus, allowing specialization of regions and one-way flow. Ruminants (cows) have a four-chambered stomach with symbiotic microbes to digest cellulose.

Reproduction

Asexual reproduction: budding (hydra), fragmentation (sea stars), parthenogenesis (development from unfertilized egg, some reptiles and insects). Sexual reproduction involves meiosis and fusion of gametes. External fertilization occurs in water (most fish, amphibians). Internal fertilization occurs on land (reptiles, birds, mammals) and requires less gamete production but more parental investment.

11 — Ecology

Population Ecology

Population size is affected by births, deaths, immigration, and emigration. Exponential growth (J-curve) occurs with unlimited resources: dN/dt = rN. Logistic growth (S-curve) accounts for carrying capacity (K): dN/dt = rN(K-N)/K. The population levels off near K due to density-dependent factors (competition, predation, disease).

Exponential: dN/dt = rN | Logistic: dN/dt = rN((K - N) / K)

r-selected species produce many offspring with little investment (insects, bacteria). K-selected species produce few offspring with high investment (elephants, whales). Survivorship curves: Type I (high survival until old age, humans), Type II (constant mortality, songbirds), Type III (high early mortality, oysters).

Competitive exclusion principle: two species cannot occupy the same niche indefinitely. Resource partitioning allows similar species to coexist by using slightly different resources. Keystone species have a disproportionately large effect on community structure relative to their abundance (e.g., sea otters controlling sea urchin populations).

Energy Flow

Energy flows through ecosystems in one direction. Producers (autotrophs) fix energy from sunlight. Primary consumers eat producers, secondary consumers eat primary consumers, and so on. Only about 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels (10% rule). Decomposers recycle nutrients at every level. A food web is more realistic than a food chain because most organisms eat and are eaten by multiple species.

10% Rule: Of energy consumed, ~10% is passed to the next trophic level; the rest is lost as heat via cellular respiration.

Biodiversity Threats

The major threats to biodiversity can be remembered by HIPPO: Habitat loss (the single greatest threat), Invasive species, Pollution, Population growth (human), and Overexploitation. Habitat fragmentation reduces connectivity and increases edge effects.

HIPPO: Habitat loss, Invasive species, Pollution, Population (human), Overexploitation
