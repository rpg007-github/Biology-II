01Evolution & Natural Selection

Evolution is the change in allele frequencies in a population over time. It is the unifying theme of biology and explains both the unity and diversity of life. Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace independently proposed natural selection as the mechanism driving adaptive evolution.

Darwin's Key Observations

Individuals in a population vary in their traits, and many of these variations are heritable. All species produce more offspring than can survive, leading to a struggle for existence. Individuals whose traits give them an advantage in their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those traits to the next generation. Over time, this differential reproductive success changes the composition of the population.

Evidence for Evolution

Evidence TypeWhat It Shows
Fossil recordDocuments changes in species over time; shows transitional forms linking major groups
Homologous structuresSimilar anatomy in different species due to shared ancestry (e.g., forelimbs of mammals)
Analogous structuresSimilar function but different origin due to convergent evolution (e.g., wings of bats and insects)

Direct observationEvolution observed in real time: antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, Darwin's finches
Key distinction: Homologous structures share a common ancestor (divergent evolution). Analogous structures evolved independently under similar selective pressures (convergent evolution). Only homologous structures are useful for building phylogenies.

Artificial selection is the intentional breeding by humans for desired traits. Dog breeds, crop plants, and livestock are all products of artificial selection, and Darwin used this as an analogy for natural selection.

02Evolution of Populations

Evolution acts on populations, not individuals. A population's gene pool is the total collection of alleles in that population. Microevolution is a change in allele frequencies from one generation to the next.

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

The Hardy-Weinberg principle states that allele and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation in a population that is not evolving. This serves as a null model: if a population deviates from Hardy-Weinberg expectations, evolution is occurring.

Hardy-Weinberg Equations:
Allele frequencies: p + q = 1
Genotype frequencies: p² + 2pq + q² = 1
where p = frequency of dominant allele, q = frequency of recessive allele, p² = homozygous dominant, 2pq = heterozygous, q² = homozygous recessive.

Five Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

The population must be very large (no genetic drift), have no migration (no gene flow), have no mutations, have random mating, and have no natural selection. If any condition is violated, the population will evolve.

Mnemonic (violations that cause evolution): "Darwinian Mechanics Might Not Stop" = Drift, Mutation, Migration (gene flow), Nonrandom mating, Selection.

Mechanisms of Evolution

MechanismDescriptionEffect
Natural selectionDifferential survival and reproduction based on fitnessOnly mechanism that is adaptive (increases fitness)
Genetic driftRandom changes in allele frequency due to chanceGreater effect in small populations; reduces variation
Gene flowMovement of alleles between populations via migrationTends to reduce differences between populations
MutationChanges in DNA sequence creating new allelesUltimate source of all new genetic variation
Nonrandom matingMate choice based on phenotype (e.g., sexual selection)Changes genotype frequencies but not allele frequencies alone

Genetic Drift

Genetic drift has a disproportionate effect on small populations. Two special cases are the bottleneck effect, where a drastic reduction in population size causes a loss of genetic variation (e.g., cheetahs, Northern elephant seals), and the founder effect, where a small group colonizes a new area carrying only a fraction of the original gene pool (e.g., Amish populations and Ellis-van Creveld syndrome).

Key point: Natural selection is the only mechanism of evolution that consistently leads to adaptive change. Genetic drift, gene flow, and mutation can cause evolution, but the changes are not necessarily adaptive.

03Speciation & History of Life

A species, under the biological species concept, is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring in nature. Speciation is the formation of new species, and it requires reproductive isolation between populations.

Reproductive Barriers

CategoryBarrierDescription
Prezygotic (before fertilization)Habitat isolationSpecies live in different habitats in the same area
Temporal isolationSpecies breed at different times (seasons, times of day)
Behavioral isolationSpecies have different courtship rituals or signals
Mechanical isolationStructural differences prevent mating
Gametic isolationGametes are incompatible and cannot fuse
Postzygotic (after fertilization)Reduced hybrid viabilityHybrid embryos do not develop properly
Reduced hybrid fertilityHybrids are viable but sterile (e.g., mule)
Hybrid breakdownFirst generation hybrids are fertile, but subsequent generations are weak or sterile

Modes of Speciation

Allopatric speciation occurs when populations are geographically separated by a physical barrier (mountain range, river, ocean). Reproductive isolation develops during separation. Sympatric speciation occurs without geographic separation, often through polyploidy in plants (autopolyploidy from the same species or allopolyploidy from hybridization between species) or habitat differentiation.

Polyploidy is the most common mechanism of sympatric speciation in plants. An autopolyploid has more than two chromosome sets from a single species (e.g., 4n from failed meiosis). An allopolyploid has chromosome sets from two different species, formed by hybridization followed by chromosome doubling.

Patterns in the History of Life

Adaptive radiation is the rapid diversification of a single lineage into many species filling diverse ecological roles, often after a mass extinction or colonization of new habitat (e.g., Darwin's finches, Hawaiian silverswords, mammals after dinosaur extinction). Punctuated equilibrium describes a pattern of long periods of stasis interrupted by brief episodes of rapid speciation, contrasting with the gradualism model of steady, continuous change.

04Phylogeny & Systematics

Phylogeny is the evolutionary history of a species or group of species. Systematics is the discipline that classifies organisms and determines their evolutionary relationships. Phylogenetic trees are diagrams that represent these relationships.

Reading Phylogenetic Trees

The tips (terminal nodes) represent taxa (species or groups). Internal nodes (branch points) represent common ancestors. Sister taxa share the most recent common ancestor. The root represents the oldest ancestor in the tree. The pattern of branching, not the length of branches or the order of taxa on the tips, indicates relatedness.

Common misconception: Taxa that are next to each other on a tree are not necessarily most closely related. You must trace back to their most recent common ancestor. Branches can be rotated at any node without changing the relationships.

05Prokaryotes

Prokaryotes are the most abundant and metabolically diverse organisms on Earth. They lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. The two domains of prokaryotes, Bacteria and Archaea, are fundamentally different despite superficial similarities.

Prokaryotic Cell Structure

All prokaryotes have a plasma membrane, cytoplasm with ribosomes (70S), a nucleoid region with circular DNA, and most have a cell wall. Many also have flagella for motility, pili for attachment or conjugation, and a capsule or slime layer for protection.

Gram Staining

Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan wall and stain purple/violet. Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan wall plus an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharides (LPS) and stain pink/red. Gram-negative bacteria are generally more resistant to antibiotics because the outer membrane acts as an additional barrier.

Genetic Recombination in Prokaryotes

Prokaryotes reproduce asexually by binary fission, but they can gain genetic diversity through three mechanisms: transformation (uptake of free DNA from the environment), transduction (transfer of DNA by bacteriophages), and conjugation (direct transfer of DNA through a pilus, often carrying plasmids with antibiotic resistance genes).

Ecological importance: Prokaryotes are essential decomposers and nutrient cyclers. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria convert atmospheric N₂ to ammonia (NH₃) that plants can use. Cyanobacteria are major oxygen producers. Gut bacteria aid in digestion and vitamin synthesis. Prokaryotes are used in bioremediation, food production, and biotechnology.

06Protists

Protists are eukaryotes that are not plants, animals, or fungi. "Protista" is not a true clade but a polyphyletic grouping of diverse eukaryotic lineages. Protists can be unicellular, colonial, or multicellular, and they display enormous diversity in nutrition, reproduction, and habitat.

Endosymbiotic Theory

Mitochondria evolved from an aerobic alpha-proteobacterium engulfed by a host cell. Chloroplasts evolved from a photosynthetic cyanobacterium engulfed by a eukaryotic cell. Evidence includes the double membranes, their own circular DNA, 70S ribosomes, and binary fission-like division.

Key evidence for endosymbiosis: Both mitochondria and chloroplasts have double membranes (inner from the engulfed cell, outer from the host vacuole), their own circular DNA, their own ribosomes (70S, bacterial-type), and they replicate by a fission process independent of the cell cycle.

07Land Plants & Seed Plants

Plants evolved from green algae (charophytes are the closest living relatives). The move to land required adaptations to prevent desiccation, provide structural support, transport water, and reproduce without standing water.

Alternation of Generations

All land plants alternate between a multicellular haploid stage (gametophyte, produces gametes by mitosis) and a multicellular diploid stage (sporophyte, produces spores by meiosis). The trend in plant evolution is a reduction of the gametophyte and increasing dominance of the sporophyte.

Trend: Bryophytes = dominant gametophyte, small sporophyte. Ferns = dominant sporophyte, independent gametophyte. Seed plants = dominant sporophyte, microscopic gametophyte (pollen grain and embryo sac).

Angiosperm Specifics

Angiosperms undergo double fertilization: one sperm fuses with the egg to form the diploid zygote (2n), and a second sperm fuses with the two polar nuclei to form the triploid endosperm (3n), which nourishes the developing embryo. The flower is the reproductive structure. Sepals protect the bud, petals attract pollinators, stamens produce pollen (male), and the carpel (pistil) contains the ovule (female). The ovary develops into the fruit after fertilization.

Remember double fertilization: "One sperm + egg = zygote (2n). Second sperm + 2 polar nuclei = endosperm (3n)." Only angiosperms do this.

08Fungi

Fungi are heterotrophic eukaryotes that digest food externally by secreting enzymes and then absorbing the nutrients (absorptive heterotrophy). They have cell walls made of chitin, not cellulose. Most fungi are multicellular and composed of threadlike hyphae that form a mycelium. Fungi are more closely related to animals than to plants.

Fungal Structure

A hypha is a filamentous cell or chain of cells. The entire network of hyphae is called the mycelium. Septate hyphae have cross-walls (septa) between cells; coenocytic hyphae lack septa and are essentially multinucleate tubes. The large surface area of the mycelium is ideal for absorption.

Ecological Roles of Fungi

Fungi serve as decomposers (breaking down dead organic matter and recycling nutrients, especially carbon and nitrogen), mutualists (mycorrhizae with plant roots enhance nutrient uptake; lichens are a mutualism between a fungus and a photosynthetic partner), and parasites/pathogens (athlete's foot, ringworm, Dutch elm disease, chytrid fungus devastating amphibians worldwide).

Mycorrhizae are mutualistic associations between fungi and plant roots. The fungus receives sugars from the plant and provides the plant with increased water and mineral absorption (especially phosphorus). About 80-90% of all plant species form mycorrhizal associations. The two main types are ectomycorrhizae (fungal hyphae surround root cells) and arbuscular mycorrhizae (fungal hyphae penetrate root cell walls).
Lichens are a mutualistic association between a fungus (usually ascomycete) and a photosynthetic partner (green alga or cyanobacterium). The fungus provides structure and protection; the photosynthetic partner provides food. Lichens are pioneer species that can colonize bare rock.

09Animal Diversity & Invertebrates

Animals are multicellular, heterotrophic eukaryotes that lack cell walls and obtain nutrients by ingestion. Most animals have some form of nervous and muscle tissue. Animals are thought to have evolved from choanoflagellate-like ancestors.

10Vertebrates

The Amniotic Egg

The amniotic egg was a key adaptation for life on land, allowing reptiles, birds, and mammals to reproduce without returning to water. Its four extraembryonic membranes are the amnion (fluid-filled sac cushioning the embryo), the chorion (gas exchange), the allantois (waste storage), and the yolk sac (nutrition).

Ectotherm vs. Endotherm: Ectotherms (fish, amphibians, most reptiles) rely on external heat sources to regulate body temperature. Endotherms (birds, mammals) generate metabolic heat internally. Endothermy allows sustained high activity but requires much more food.

11Ecology

Ecology is the scientific study of interactions between organisms and their environment. It operates at multiple levels: organismal, population, community, ecosystem, and biosphere.

Population Ecology

A population is a group of individuals of the same species in the same area at the same time. Population size is affected by births, deaths, immigration, and emigration.

ConceptDefinition / Formula
Population densityNumber of individuals per unit area or volume
Exponential growthdN/dt = rN; J-shaped curve; unlimited resources
Logistic growthdN/dt = rN(K-N)/K; S-shaped curve; includes carrying capacity (K)
Carrying capacity (K)Maximum population size the environment can sustain
r-selected speciesHigh reproductive rate, little parental care, small body, short lifespan (e.g., insects, bacteria)
K-selected speciesLow reproductive rate, high parental care, large body, long lifespan (e.g., elephants, whales)
Density-dependent factorsCompetition, predation, disease (effects increase as population grows)
Density-independent factorsWeather, natural disasters, habitat destruction (unrelated to population size)
Exponential vs. Logistic: Exponential growth assumes unlimited resources (J-curve). Logistic growth incorporates a carrying capacity K and slows as the population approaches K (S-curve). In nature, most populations approximate logistic growth.

Community Ecology

A community is all the populations of different species in an area. Species interactions shape community structure.

InteractionEffect (Species 1 / Species 2)Example

The competitive exclusion principle states that two species competing for the same limited resource cannot coexist indefinitely; one will outcompete the other. This drives resource partitioning, where species divide resources to reduce competition. A keystone species has a disproportionately large impact on its community relative to its abundance (e.g., sea otters, sea stars).

Ecosystem Ecology

An ecosystem includes all organisms in an area plus the abiotic (nonliving) environment. Energy flows through ecosystems in one direction (sunlight to producers to consumers to decomposers), while nutrients are recycled.

Trophic LevelRoleExamples
Primary producersConvert solar energy to chemical energy (photosynthesis)Plants, algae, cyanobacteria
Primary consumersHerbivores that eat producersRabbits, zooplankton, grasshoppers
Secondary consumersCarnivores that eat herbivoresFrogs, small fish
Tertiary consumersTop carnivoresHawks, sharks, large cats

10% Rule: Only about 10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next. The rest is lost as heat through cellular respiration. This is why food chains are typically limited to 4-5 levels and why there are far fewer top predators than producers.

Biogeochemical Cycles

The water cycle moves water through evaporation, transpiration, precipitation, and runoff. The carbon cycle moves carbon through photosynthesis (CO₂ to organic molecules), cellular respiration (organic molecules to CO₂), decomposition, and fossil fuel combustion. The nitrogen cycle converts atmospheric N₂ to usable forms through nitrogen fixation (bacteria), nitrification, assimilation by plants, and denitrification back to N₂.

Conservation Ecology

Biodiversity is threatened by habitat destruction (the single greatest threat), invasive species, overexploitation, pollution, and climate change. Biodiversity has three levels: genetic diversity (within species), species diversity (among species), and ecosystem diversity (variety of ecosystems). Conservation strategies include establishing protected areas, habitat corridors, captive breeding, and addressing the underlying causes of biodiversity loss.

Threats to biodiversity (HIPPO): Habitat loss, Invasive species, Pollution, Population growth (human), Overexploitation.
